# Humans

Humans are the most widespread and politically dominant people on [Arkhaven](../Locations/Continents/Arkhaven.md), but they are not considered native to the continent.

Human histories describe their arrival by sea aboard a small fleet of arks, expeditionary ships, and survival vessels. These ships carried several hundred sailors, settlers, priests, soldiers, craftspeople, and scholars from an older homeland beyond the horizon.

The name **Arkhaven** is human in origin.

To the earliest settlers, the continent was a haven reached by ark.

## Overview

Humans are highly adaptable, socially diverse, and politically divided.

They live across nearly every major settled region of Arkhaven, including coastal territories, river valleys, central grasslands, trade towns, religious centres, frontier settlements, and fortified states.

Human societies are not unified under a single ruler, crown, faith, or culture.

Instead, they are divided into numerous kingdoms, republics, theocracies, city-states, marcher territories, trade leagues, and independent settlements.

Humans are commonly associated with:

- Rapid population growth
- Political ambition
- Settlement expansion
- Agriculture
- Road building
- Religious organisation
- Trade networks
- Military adaptability
- Cultural assimilation
- Territorial dispute

Their influence is not based on age or ancient continuity, but on growth, organisation, and expansion.

## Origins

The first humans arrived in Arkhaven after the formation of [The Godscar](../Lore/Arkhaven%20Events/The%20Godscar.md) but before [The Green Silence](../Lore/Arkhaven%20Events/The%20Green%20Silence.md).

The old human homeland has not yet been fully defined in surviving Arkhaven records.

Most human traditions agree that the voyage began after warnings of a foretold disaster, though the nature of that disaster differs between sources.

Some accounts describe divine warning.

Others describe famine, war, plague, political collapse, celestial omen, or environmental ruin.

The earliest human accounts are inconsistent and were often recorded generations after the landing.

No human record is old enough to preserve direct knowledge of the true origin of the Godscar.

## The Arkfall

The arrival of the first humans is commonly called **The Arkfall**.

The term refers to both the physical landing of the first ark-ships and the beginning of permanent human settlement on Arkhaven.

The first arrivals are often called **the Arkborne**.

Later noble houses claiming descent from the first captains, priests, and shipmasters are sometimes referred to as **Arkline Houses**.

The Arkfall is treated differently by different cultures.

Human states often describe it as an act of survival, courage, and divine providence.

Older established peoples and local traditions may describe it more critically, as the beginning of colonisation, displacement, and political disruption.

## Early Settlement

The first human settlements were established along coastlines, river mouths, sheltered bays, and fertile lowland regions.

Early communities depended heavily on:

- Fishing
- Ship repair
- Coastal farming
- Hunting
- Trade with established local peoples
- Salvage from the ark-ships
- Religious organisation
- Defensive cooperation

At first, human populations were small and vulnerable.

Their survival depended on adaptation, alliances, and access to food, timber, stone, and fresh water.

Over time, the settlements expanded inland.

## Expansion

Human expansion across Arkhaven occurred gradually over several centuries.

The main drivers of expansion were:

- Population growth
- Agricultural settlement
- Timber demand
- Road construction
- Religious mission
- Trade development
- Territorial ambition
- Access to mines and rivers
- Control of strategic crossings

Humans were not stronger than every people they encountered.

They were more numerous, more expansionist, and more willing to settle contested or unstable land.

This allowed them to become the dominant population across much of the continent.

## Colonial Legacy

The rise of human power on Arkhaven is inseparable from colonial settlement.

Human societies often portray their history as one of survival and civilisation-building.

Many non-human cultures remember the same process as displacement, treaty-breaking, forced conversion, land seizure, cultural absorption, and exploitation.

Both views exist in the modern continent.

Many human families have now lived on Arkhaven for centuries and consider themselves fully native to the land.

At the same time, older peoples often preserve records showing that many human towns, farms, roads, and temples were built on land previously held, used, or protected by others.

This tension remains one of the major unresolved political and cultural issues in Arkhaven.

## Political Division

There is no single human ruler of Arkhaven.

Human populations are divided among many states and systems of government.

Common forms include:

- Monarchies
- Theocracies
- Merchant republics
- Free cities
- Military marcher states
- Agrarian kingdoms
- Settler leagues
- Noble confederations

Some rulers claim descent from the Arkborne.

Others base legitimacy on conquest, election, wealth, military control, religious authority, or popular support.

Human states frequently compete with one another for territory, trade routes, religious influence, access to rivers, and control of strategic crossings.

## Society

Human societies vary greatly by region.

Despite this variation, several common patterns are widespread.

Most human settlements place strong emphasis on:

- Family inheritance
- Land ownership
- Written law
- Taxation
- Farming
- Military service
- Market trade
- Religious participation
- Civic identity

Human society is generally more mobile and less bound by ancient custom than elven or dwarven society.

This flexibility has allowed humans to adapt quickly to new regions, institutions, and political structures.

It has also made human societies prone to instability, factionalism, and rapid ideological change.

## Religion

Religion plays a major role in human culture.

Human churches were strongly shaped by the fact that humans arrived after the Godscar had already formed.

Because humans did not witness the original event, their religious traditions developed through interpretation, adoption, dispute, and revision.

Many human-led churches treated the Godscar as evidence that Arkhaven was divinely significant.

Some went further and argued that the arrival of the Arkborne had been foretold or divinely sanctioned.

These claims later became politically important.

Human rulers and churches often used religious authority to justify settlement, territorial claims, law, and war.

## War of the False Saints

Humans were major participants in [War of the False Saints](../Lore/Arkhaven%20Events/War%20of%20the%20False%20Saints.md).

They were not the only people involved, but human-led churches and states were among the main accelerants of the conflict.

Several factors contributed to this:

- Rapidly expanding religious institutions
- Young and inconsistent church records
- Political competition between human states
- Desire for divine legitimacy
- Use of saints and relics to justify land claims
- Popular response to miracle claims and prophecy

The war exposed the dangers of religious ambition combined with unstable historical memory.

It also contributed directly to the later authority of [The Scriptor Compact](../Lore/Arkhaven%20Events/The%20Scriptor%20Compact.md).

## Relationship with the Scriptor Compact

The Scriptor Compact is often viewed as a response to the instability of human religious institutions.

Many human churches accepted Scriptors because doing so provided legitimacy, continuity, and access to older records.

At the same time, many human clergy and rulers resent the influence of long-lived elven advisors within their institutions.

This creates an ongoing tension.

Human churches need Scriptor authority, but often dislike Scriptor oversight.

## Relationship with Elves

Human relations with [Elves](Elves.md) are complex.

Elves often view humans as impatient, expansionist, historically careless, and politically volatile.

Humans often view elves as secretive, obstructive, arrogant, and unwilling to share knowledge.

Despite this, cooperation is common.

Human rulers rely on elven scholars, translators, Scriptors, diplomats, and magical specialists.

Elven institutions often rely on human roads, trade systems, military protection, and political access.

## Relationship with Dwarves

Human relations with [Dwarves](Dwarves.md) are generally practical and economically important.

Humans rely heavily on dwarven expertise in:

- Stonework
- Metalwork
- Engineering
- Mining
- Fortification
- Bridge construction
- Tunnel surveying

Disputes occur over mining rights, mountain borders, taxation, and control of trade routes.

However, human and dwarven states maintain some of the strongest economic relationships on the continent.

## Relationship with Tabaxi

Human relations with [Tabaxi](Tabaxi.md) are more strained in many northern regions.

Some human traders, hunters, and criminal groups have been involved in attempts to capture young tabaxi for sale.

Many human states officially condemn such practices.

Others have failed to prevent them, ignored them, or allowed illegal markets to operate through corruption.

As a result, tabaxi communities often treat human travellers with caution until trust is earned.

## Reputation

Humans have a divided reputation across Arkhaven.

They are often regarded as:

- Adaptable
- Ambitious
- Energetic
- Inventive
- Greedy
- Expansionist
- Brave
- Restless
- Politically divided
- Religiously intense
- Quick to build
- Quick to justify conquest

These reputations vary by region and class.

A human farmer, sailor, saint, soldier, merchant, priest, and colonising noble may have little in common beyond shared ancestry and broad cultural inheritance.

## Strengths

Human strengths are generally social and institutional rather than biological.

Humans are capable of rapid organisation, settlement, alliance-building, and cultural adaptation.

They are often willing to adopt useful practices from other peoples and reshape them into human institutions.

This has made them effective builders of towns, roads, fleets, churches, armies, and states.

## Weaknesses

The main weaknesses of human societies are also institutional.

Human states are prone to:

- Expansionism
- Religious certainty
- Historical revision
- Political factionalism
- Short-term ambition
- Resource exploitation
- Border conflict
- Justification of conquest

Their shorter lives and fast political cycles make long-term memory difficult to preserve without archives, law, or outside institutions.

## Current Status

Humans are now the dominant population across much of Arkhaven.

They control many of the continent’s largest cities, farmland regions, trade routes, ports, churches, and armies.

However, their power is fragmented.

No single human state controls the continent, and human rulers are often as dangerous to one another as they are to neighbouring peoples.

Their history remains politically contested.

To many humans, they are the descendants of desperate survivors who crossed the sea and built a new home.

To many older peoples, they are settlers who became rulers and then wrote the map in their own language.

The central human legacy in Arkhaven is therefore one of survival becoming entitlement.
