# Myrkul

#lore #deity #religion #pantheon #arkhaven

**Myrkul** is a deity associated with fear of death, decay, old age, dread, graves, and the shadow cast by mortality.

In [[Arkhaven]], the worship of Myrkul is part of the wider recognised pantheon followed by the continent's major churches, temples, household shrines, cults, and local traditions. The form that worship takes can vary greatly between regions. A grand temple in a city, a roadside shrine near [[The Godscar]], and a whispered prayer in a frightened village may all honour the same god in very different ways.

## Portfolio

Myrkul's commonly recognised concerns include **fear of death, decay, old age, dread, graves, and the shadow cast by mortality**.

Their church, cult, or priesthood will usually frame these ideas through local needs. In one region, the faith may be public, formal, and politically powerful. In another, it may be small, practical, feared, or half-hidden.

## Worshippers

Myrkul is most often honoured by death cults, necromancers, the terrified, and those who would rather bargain with death than accept it.

Not all worship is devotion. Some prayers are acts of love, loyalty, gratitude, or discipline. Others are appeasement. In Arkhaven, many people pray to gods they do not personally admire because fear, custom, profession, or necessity demands it.

## Place in Arkhaven

The faith of Myrkul should be treated as part of the living religious landscape of Arkhaven rather than as a single, fixed institution. Different temples may disagree over doctrine. Rural shrines may preserve older practices that city priests consider embarrassing or heretical. Local saints, relics, taboos, pilgrim routes, and holy days may vary from place to place.

This matters because the churches of Arkhaven are shaped by history. [[The Godscar]] gave the continent a divine wound that every religion has tried to explain. [[War of the False Saints]] taught people that miracles, relics, visions, and saints could be misused. [[The Green Silence]] proved that whole histories can vanish beneath root, moss, and silence.

A temple of Myrkul may therefore be sincere, corrupt, cautious, divided, or all of those at once.

## Scriptors and the Church

Where the worship of Myrkul is recognised as a major church, its highest-ranking religious authority may be assigned an elven advisor under [[The Scriptor Compact]].

Such a Scriptor would preserve records, compare doctrine, inspect disputed miracles, advise on magical matters, and keep the church from drifting too far from its own history. In more severe branches of the faith, the Scriptor may also serve as an Inquisitor-Scriptor, investigating heresy, forged relics, false prophecy, or magical manipulation.

Whether a Scriptor assigned to Myrkul's church is welcomed, tolerated, or feared will depend on the local faith.

## Links to the War of the False Saints

During [[War of the False Saints]], the name of Myrkul may have been invoked by true believers, frightened communities, political opportunists, or deliberate frauds. This does not mean the deity endorsed every act committed in their name.

The central lesson of that age still applies: a god may be real, a church may be sincere, and a miracle may still need a witness.

## Common Adventure Uses

- A temple of Myrkul asks the party to investigate a disputed miracle.
- A local shrine claims an older and more authentic tradition than the official church.
- A Scriptor refuses to endorse a new doctrine connected to Myrkul.
- A relic associated with Myrkul is found near [[The Godscar]].
- A cult twists one of Myrkul's teachings into something dangerous.
- A priest of Myrkul believes their church condemned a true saint during [[War of the False Saints]].
- A forgotten ruin from before [[The Green Silence]] contains a record that changes how the faith understands itself.

## Notes for the DM

Myrkul's worship should not be treated as monolithic. Let regional practice, local history, and political pressure shape the faith. The same god can inspire charity in one place, fear in another, reform in another, and abuse somewhere else.

This keeps religion in Arkhaven flexible, useful, and dangerous without needing every temple to represent the whole faith.
